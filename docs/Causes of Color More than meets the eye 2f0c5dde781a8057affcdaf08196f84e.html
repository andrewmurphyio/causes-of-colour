Causes of Color: More than meets the eye

Opening Hook: The Eye Color Question

Start the talk by asking the audience: "Why are brown eyes brown, and blue eyes blue?"

Most people assume it's the same answer ‚Äî pigment. But it's not.

  • Brown eyes: Melanin pigment absorbs blue light ‚Üí chemistry
  • Blue eyes: Zero blue pigment. Light scatters off collagen fibers in the stroma ‚Üí physics

Same organ. Completely different mechanisms. Grind up a blue iris and you get brownish mush ‚Äî there's no blue pigment to extract.

This is the central insight of the talk: We perceive color as a single sensation, but it emerges from radically different physical processes.

The Tentpole: Nature's Color Champion

The common cuttlefish (Sepia officinalis) deploys more simultaneous color-production mechanisms than any other documented animal.

This single creature uses at least 4 of Nassau's 15 mechanisms working in concert ‚Äî a living demonstration of nearly everything we'll cover in this talk.

How It Works Together

The chromatophores are the magic trick. Each one has 10-30 radial muscle fibers that can expand or contract in milliseconds. When expanded, the pigment spreads thin and filters light passing through. When contracted, it's nearly invisible.

Think of it as a biological pixel display:

  • Chromatophores = RGB subpixels (absorptive filters)
  • Iridophores = metallic/iridescent layer (adds shimmer)
  • Leucophores = white backlight (reflects ambient light)

By selectively expanding different chromatophore combinations over different structural layers, the cuttlefish can produce virtually any color or pattern ‚Äî and change it in 200 milliseconds.

Why This Matters for the Talk

The cuttlefish shows that these aren't just academic categories. Evolution has discovered that layering mechanisms creates possibilities no single mechanism can achieve:

  • Pigments alone: limited palette, no iridescence
  • Interference alone: angle-dependent, washed out without absorption
  • Scattering alone: just white
  • Combined: dynamic camouflage, communication, and display
