Category 2: Light Absorbed - Chemical Mechanisms (Mechanisms 4-11)

Overview

Colors created when electrons in materials absorb specific wavelengths and we see what's left. This is the largest category — 8 distinct mechanisms.

Mechanisms covered:

Mechanism 4: Transition Metal Compounds

The Physics

Transition metals have partially filled d-orbitals. When surrounded by other atoms (ligands), these orbitals split into different energy levels. Light absorption causes d-d transitions.

Key insight: The same metal ion produces different colors depending on its chemical environment.

Non-Biological Examples

  • Copper compounds:
    • Copper sulfate (CuSO₄·5H₂O) — Blue
    • Malachite Cu₂CO₃(OH)₂ — Green
    • Turquoise CuAl₆(PO₄)₄(OH)₈·4H₂O — Blue-green
  • Chromium compounds:
    • Potassium dichromate — Orange
    • Chromium oxide — Green
  • Cobalt compounds:
    • Cobalt chloride — Blue (dry) / Pink (hydrated)
    • Cobalt aluminate — "Cobalt blue" pigment
  • Iron compounds:
    • Rust (Fe₂O₃) — Red-brown

Biological Examples

  • Hemocyanin — Copper-based oxygen carrier in octopi, squid, crustaceans, spiders → blue blood
  • Tunichromes in sea squirts — Vanadium compounds (green)
  • Chlorocruorin — Green blood in some marine worms (iron-based but different structure)

Talk Hook

Those silica gel packets with blue/pink beads? Cobalt chloride. Blue = dry, pink = absorbed moisture. Chemistry you can see!

Key Insight

Same metal, different chemical partners = completely different colors.

Mechanism 5: Transition Metal Impurities

The Physics

Trace amounts of transition metal ions substituting into a crystal structure that would otherwise be colorless. The host crystal's geometry determines the exact color.

The magic: The same impurity ion creates completely different colors in different host crystals.

Non-Biological Examples

Chromium (Cr³⁺) in different hosts:

  • Ruby — Cr³⁺ in aluminum oxide (Al₂O₃) → RED
  • Emerald — Cr³⁺ in beryllium aluminum silicate → GREEN
  • Alexandrite — Cr³⁺ in chrysoberyl → Red in incandescent, green in daylight

Other impurity examples:

  • Yellow sapphire — Fe³⁺ in Al₂O₃
  • Peridot — Fe²⁺ in olivine → Yellow-green
  • Aquamarine — Fe²⁺ in beryl → Blue

Biological Examples

  • None — This is purely a geological/mineralogical mechanism. Organisms don't grow mineral crystals with controlled impurities.

Talk Hook

Ruby and emerald are both colored by chromium! A jeweler selling "green rubies" or "red emeralds" would technically be describing the chemistry more accurately.

Key Insight

Same impurity atom + different crystal host = opposite colors (ruby red vs emerald green).

Mechanism 6: Organic Compounds

The Physics

Large conjugated molecules (alternating single and double bonds) have delocalized π-electrons that can absorb visible light.

The rule: More conjugated double bonds → absorbs longer wavelengths → appears as complementary color.

Non-Biological Examples

  • Indigo — The blue of blue jeans. One of the oldest known dyes.
  • Alizarin — Red dye from madder root
  • Synthetic food dyes — Engineered conjugation lengths
  • Mauveine — First synthetic dye (1856), launched the chemical industry

Biological Examples

  • Chlorophyll — Absorbs red and blue, reflects green. Porphyrin ring + magnesium.
  • Hemoglobin — Porphyrin ring + iron. Oxygenated = bright red, deoxygenated = darker red (not blue!)
  • Carotenoids:
    • β-carotene — Orange (carrots, sweet potatoes)
    • Lycopene — Red (tomatoes, watermelon)
    • Xanthophylls — Yellow (egg yolks, autumn leaves)
  • Melanin — Brown/black in skin, hair, eyes, feathers
  • Anthocyanins — Red/purple/blue in flowers, berries. pH sensitive!
  • Bilirubin — Yellow in bruises and jaundice
  • Psittacofulvins — Red/yellow pigments unique to parrots

Talk Hook

Flamingos are born grey! They turn pink from carotenoids in shrimp and algae they eat. Zoo flamingos on carotenoid-free diets fade to white. You literally are what you eat.

Key Insight

Autumn leaves: Chlorophyll breaks down → reveals yellow carotenoids that were there all along → some trees synthesize red anthocyanins.

Mechanism 7: Charge Transfer

The Physics

An electron transfers between two different atoms/ions, typically from a donor to an acceptor. This requires much more energy than d-d transitions, producing very intense colors.

Key difference from mechanism 5: Electron moves between atoms, not within one atom's orbitals.

Non-Biological Examples

  • Blue sapphire — Fe²⁺ + Ti⁴⁺ impurities in Al₂O₃. Electron transfers: Fe²⁺ → Ti⁴⁺, absorbs yellow/orange. Neither iron nor titanium alone produces blue!
  • Lapis lazuli / Ultramarine — S₃⁻ radical ions trapped in silicate cage
  • Prussian blue — Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺ through cyanide bridges. First modern synthetic pigment (1704).
  • Crocoite (lead chromate) — O → Cr charge transfer, intense orange

Biological Examples

  • Blue copper proteins — Plastocyanin, azurin (electron transport)
  • Iron-sulfur proteins — Brown color from charge transfer
  • Phycocyanin in cyanobacteria — Charge transfer contributes to intense blue

Talk Hook

Blue sapphire requires two specific impurities sitting next to each other — iron AND titanium. Remove either one, colorless. It's a two-person chemical act.

Key Insight

Charge transfer colors are intensely vivid because the transition is "allowed" quantum mechanically.

Mechanism 8: Metals

The Physics

Metals have "free" electrons in a conduction band. Most metals reflect all visible wavelengths equally = silver/grey. But some have interband transitions where electrons jump to higher bands, absorbing specific wavelengths.

Non-Biological Examples

  • Gold — Absorbs blue/violet light, reflects yellow/red. Relativistic effects make gold golden!
  • Copper — Absorbs into green, reflects orange-red
  • Silver, aluminum, platinum, steel — No interband transitions in visible range → silvery-grey
  • Gold nanoparticles — Appear red (plasmon resonance, different physics)

Biological Examples

  • None — Organisms don't produce metallic materials with these properties
  • Note: Beetles with metallic appearance use structural color (mechanism 14), not metal physics

Talk Hook

Gold is golden because of relativity! Gold's 79 protons accelerate electrons to relativistic speeds, shifting absorption into visible range. Without relativity, gold would be silver-colored.

Key Insight

Only gold, copper, and a few alloys are naturally "colored" metals — everything else is silver/grey.

Mechanism 9: Pure Semiconductors

The Physics

Electrons jump from valence band to conduction band. The "band gap" energy determines which wavelengths are absorbed.

Non-Biological Examples

  • Cinnabar (HgS) — Band gap ~2 eV, absorbs blue/green → vermillion red
  • Cadmium sulfide (CdS) — Band gap ~2.4 eV → "Cadmium yellow"
  • Cadmium selenide (CdSe) — Smaller band gap → "Cadmium red"
  • Silicon — Band gap 1.1 eV (infrared), absorbs ALL visible → grey/metallic
  • Diamond — Band gap 5.5 eV (deep UV), absorbs nothing visible → colorless

Biological Examples

  • None — Semiconductor physics doesn't occur in biological systems

Talk Hook

Vermillion (cinnabar) was so prized in ancient Rome that its price was fixed by law. Worth more than some metals — just mercury sulfide!

Key Insight

Band gap size directly determines color: small gap = absorbs everything = black/grey; large gap = absorbs nothing = colorless.

Mechanism 10: Doped Semiconductors

The Physics

Impurities create additional energy levels within the band gap, enabling new absorptions.

Non-Biological Examples

  • Yellow diamond — Nitrogen impurities (most common diamond impurity)
  • Blue diamond — Boron impurities (Hope Diamond). Incredibly rare!
  • LEDs — the modern revolution:
    • Red LED: Gallium arsenide phosphide (GaAsP)
    • Yellow/Green: GaP with nitrogen doping
    • Blue: Gallium nitride (GaN) — 2014 Nobel Prize

Biological Examples

  • None — Semiconductor doping is solid-state physics, not biology

Talk Hook

The blue LED took decades longer than red/green. Once cracked in the 1990s, suddenly: white LEDs (blue + phosphor), full-color screens, Blu-ray. One missing color held up a technological revolution.

Key Insight

Controlled impurities in semiconductors gave us the entire LED lighting and display revolution.

Mechanism 11: Color Centers

The Physics

Crystal defects — missing atoms, extra atoms, trapped electrons — create localized energy states that absorb specific wavelengths. Often caused by natural radiation over geological time.

Non-Biological Examples

  • Amethyst — Iron impurity + radiation creates Fe⁴⁺ color center. Heat it → citrine (yellow)!
  • Smoky quartz — Radiation creates [AlO₄]⁴⁻ centers with trapped holes
  • Fluorite — Nearly every color depending on impurities and radiation
  • Blue halite (salt) — Radiation creates F-centers (electron in chlorine vacancy)

Biological Examples

  • None — Color centers require crystal lattices and often geological radiation exposure

Talk Hook

Most commercial citrine is heat-treated amethyst! You're literally undoing millions of years of natural irradiation with a kiln. Same crystal, same iron, different temperature = different color center state.

Key Insight

Radiation damage + time can create beautiful gemstone colors.
