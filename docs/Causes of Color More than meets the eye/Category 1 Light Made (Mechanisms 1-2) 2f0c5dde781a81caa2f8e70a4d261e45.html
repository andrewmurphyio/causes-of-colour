Category 1: Light Made (Mechanisms 1-2)

Overview

Colors where the object generates light rather than modifying incoming light.

Mechanisms covered:

  • Mechanism 1: Incandescence
  • Mechanism 2: Gas Excitations / Luminescence

Mechanism 1: Incandescence

The Physics

Thermal radiation from hot objects. All matter above absolute zero emits electromagnetic radiation; it becomes visible light at high temperatures.

As temperature increases, peak emission wavelength shifts shorter (Wien's Law). This gives us the "color temperature" scale photographers use.

Non-Biological Examples

  • Candle flame (~1,800K) — Yellow-orange, relatively cool
  • Incandescent bulb filament (~2,700K) — Warm white/yellow
  • Molten steel (~1,500°C) — Starts cherry red, shifts to orange, then white as it heats
  • The Sun's surface (5,778K) — White (appears yellow through atmosphere)
  • Blue-white stars (10,000-30,000K) — Rigel, Sirius
  • Red giant stars (~3,500K) — Betelgeuse, Antares
  • Lava/magma — Temperature visible as color gradient

Biological Examples

  • None directly — Biology generally can't sustain combustion temperatures
  • Closest analog: Dragonfish produce far-red bioluminescence that functions similarly in being invisible to most prey

Talk Hook

Blacksmiths have used "heat colors" for centuries — cherry red (750°C), bright red (850°C), orange (1000°C), yellow (1100°C), white (1300°C). They were doing materials science by eye!

Key Insight

"Red hot" is actually cooler than "white hot" — the opposite of how we use red/blue for hot/cold taps!

Mechanism 2: Gas Excitations / Luminescence

The Physics

Electrons in atoms get excited to higher energy levels, then emit specific wavelengths when they fall back down. Unlike incandescence (continuous spectrum), this produces discrete spectral lines — each element has its own signature colors.

Non-Biological Examples

  • Neon signs — True neon gas glows red-orange. Other "neon" colors:
    • Argon = lavender
    • Helium = gold
    • Krypton = white
    • Xenon = blue
  • Sodium street lamps — Distinctive orange-yellow (589nm doublet)
  • Auroras:
    • Green (oxygen at 557nm, most common)
    • Red (oxygen at 630nm, high altitude)
    • Blue/purple (nitrogen)
  • Fireworks colors:
    • Red: strontium compounds
    • Green: barium compounds
    • Blue: copper compounds (hardest to achieve!)
    • Yellow: sodium (so easy it's hard to avoid)
    • White: magnesium or aluminum burning
  • Lightning — Nitrogen/oxygen emission, blue-white
  • Fluorescent tubes — Mercury vapor emits UV, phosphor coating converts to visible

Biological Examples

  • Fireflies — Luciferin + luciferase enzyme → excited state → photon
  • Anglerfish lure — Bioluminescent bacteria in symbiosis
  • Deep-sea creatures — 76% are bioluminescent!
  • Dinoflagellates — Glowing ocean waves
  • Bioluminescent fungi — Foxfire / glowing mushrooms
  • Jellyfish — Green fluorescent protein (GFP) — won 2008 Nobel Prize
  • Comb jellies — Rainbow iridescence + bioluminescence

Talk Hook

Fireworks engineers call copper blue the "holy grail" — it requires precise temperature control. Too hot and the copper compounds decompose before they can emit. Blue fireworks are genuinely harder to make than any other color.

Key Insight

Each gas has its own color signature — that's how we know what distant stars are made of (spectroscopy).
